Welcome!

I am a third-year Ph.D. student in Political Science at the University of Florida (Gainesville, FL). I am also a predoctoral fellow at the Violence, Conflict, and Security Lab. I received my M.A. in International Affairs from Missouri State University and a B.A. in International Studies from the American University of Iraq, Sulaimani. My research focuses on comparative politics and international security with substantive research interests in political violence, civil wars, ethnic, communal, identity, and self-determination conflicts, with an empirical focus on the Middle East and North Africa (MENA). Methodologically, I employ both quantitative and qualitative approaches.

My dissertation examines the determinants of subnational variation in communal violence. Specifically, I ask why communal violence emerges and escalates in some localities—but not in others—within divided societies. This research combines quantitative analysis of conflict data with detailed case studies from the Middle East to examine the local-level factors that enable or constrain violent mobilization across ethnic and sectarian lines.

My research interests broadly span several interconnected areas within political science. I am particularly interested in how ethnic and sectarian identities shape conflict patterns in the MENA region, with attention to the role of state institutions and local governance structures. Additionally, I examine state strategies in responding to self-determination movements and how these responses affect conflict trajectories and outcomes in contested regions.

For more information on my published and ongoing research, please see my Research Projects or my Curriculum Vitae.

Prior to my doctoral studies, I gained valuable research experience working on projects related to Middle Eastern politics and international security. When I am not researching, you can find me exploring Gainesville's coffee shops, following international football, or discussing the latest developments in Middle Eastern politics with colleagues and friends.

Research Projects

Dissertation

In Progress Dissertation

Subnational Variation in Communal Violence in Divided Societies

Expected Completion: 2027

This dissertation project investigates why communal violence emerges and escalates in some localities but not others within divided societies. Using a mixed-methods approach, I analyze the local-level factors—including demographic composition, economic conditions, institutional arrangements, and historical legacies—that enable or constrain violent mobilization across ethnic and sectarian lines. The empirical focus is on Iraq and other conflict-affected states in the Middle East, drawing on original data collection and field research.

Active Research

Working Paper

Ethnic Politics and Conflict Dynamics in MENA

Under Review

This paper examines how ethnic and sectarian identities shape conflict patterns in the Middle East and North Africa region. I pay particular attention to the role of state institutions and local governance structures in mediating ethnic relations and conflict outcomes. The analysis combines quantitative analysis of conflict data with case studies from Iraq, Syria, and Lebanon.

Research in Progress

Self-Determination Movements and State Response

Data Collection Phase

This project analyzes state strategies in responding to self-determination movements and how these responses affect conflict trajectories and outcomes in contested regions. Drawing on cases from Iraqi Kurdistan and other regions, I examine the conditions under which states pursue accommodation versus repression, and the consequences of these strategies for conflict escalation and resolution.

Research in Progress

Local Governance and Violence in Post-Conflict Settings

Early Stage

This research investigates the relationship between local governance structures and patterns of violence in post-conflict settings. Using data from Iraq and other conflict-affected states, I examine how variations in local government capacity, legitimacy, and institutional design affect the likelihood and severity of communal violence.
